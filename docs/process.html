The Process Under COLA

The Sixers tanked for four straight years and got four top-3 picks.
Under COLA, the strategy self-destructs.

What Was “The Process”?

In the NBA, the worst teams get the best draft picks. From 2013 to 2017, the Philadelphia 76ers exploited this by deliberately fielding terrible rosters — a strategy their GM Sam Hinkie called “The Process.” The payoff: four consecutive top-3 picks, including Joel Embiid (#3), Ben Simmons (#1), and Markelle Fultz (#1).

The Process worked because the NBA draft has no memory. No matter how many top picks you’ve received, you can tank again next year and get another one. There is no penalty for repeat winners.

COLA (Carry-Over Lottery Allocation) changes this. Under COLA, every non-playoff team accumulates “lottery tickets” over time — but when you win a top draft pick, your tickets are reduced. Win the #1 pick? Your tickets reset to zero. Win #3? Cut in half. This means each top pick makes the next top pick harder to get.

The question: would The Process have worked under COLA? This page applies COLA’s rules to real Sixers history to find out. Read Prof. Highley’s full analysis →

The Verdict

Under COLA, The Process is self-defeating. Each top pick erases the advantage that years of losing built. A team can’t game the system by tanking repeatedly — the mechanism has memory.

This doesn’t mean bad teams can’t rebuild. A team that genuinely struggles will accumulate tickets naturally and eventually get draft help. The difference is that deliberate, repeated tanking hits diminishing returns fast — while organic rebuilding is rewarded over time. COLA distinguishes between the two.

Highley’s Part 4 puts it simply: “The Process worked because the Sixers could tank, get a high pick, and return to the top of the lottery the following year. Under COLA, returning to the top is much harder.”
